What Is Baccarat?

Baccarat is a comparing card game played between two hands — Player and Banker. The objective is to bet on which hand will get a total closer to 9. You can bet on either side, or on a Tie. The dealer manages both hands; players don't actually receive cards or make hit/stand decisions. Once the bets are placed, the round plays out according to fixed rules.

Baccarat's reputation as a high-roller game comes from its history at Monte Carlo and Macau, where VIP baccarat tables routinely handle six-figure bets. Online, baccarat is accessible at much lower stakes — most tables start at ₹100 per hand — with the same maths and same low house edge.

Hand totals

Hand total is the sum of card values modulo 10. So 7+8 = 15 → 5. A 9+6 hand = 15 → 5. The maximum possible hand value is 9.

Round flow

  1. Players place bets — Player, Banker, or Tie (and optional side bets).
  2. Dealer deals two cards each to the Player and Banker positions, face up.
  3. If either two-card total is 8 or 9, that's a "natural" — round ends, hand closer to 9 wins.
  4. Otherwise, fixed third-card rules apply. The Player position decides first based on a rule table, then the Banker decides based on its own table (which considers the Player's third card).
  5. Final hands compared. Winning bets paid.

Why is Tie so bad? It pays only 8:1 when the true odds are closer to 9.5:1. Even at 9:1 payout (offered at some tables — uncommon), Tie still has a ~5% house edge. Skip it.

Third Card Rules — Fixed and Automatic

Baccarat has fixed drawing rules that the dealer applies mechanically. You never make any decisions.

Player rule

  • If Player two-card total is 0–5: draw a third card.
  • If Player two-card total is 6 or 7: stand.
  • If Player two-card total is 8 or 9: natural — round ends.

Banker rule

If Player stood on 6 or 7:

  • Banker draws on 0–5, stands on 6, 7, 8, 9.

If Player drew a third card, Banker's rule depends on the value of Player's third card and Banker's own two-card total — a small lookup table. Most live UIs display this automatically.

The takeaway: you don't need to memorise the table. The dealer does it. Just know that decisions are mechanical.

Baccarat "Strategy" — Just Bet Selection

Baccarat has no in-play strategy decisions. You don't hit, stand, or split. The only choice you make is which bet to place. The optimal strategy in baccarat is:

  1. Always bet Banker (1.06% house edge after commission).
  2. Never bet Tie (14.4% house edge — terrible value).
  3. Skip side bets (Player Pair, Banker Pair, etc.) — they have 10%+ house edges.

That's it. There is no "Martingale" or "Fibonacci" or "1-3-2-6" system that improves baccarat's expected value. Bet selection is the entire strategy.

"Tracking the shoe" doesn't work Many baccarat tables display elaborate scoreboards tracking past Banker/Player results — bead road, big road, big eye road, small road, cockroach road. Players try to spot patterns. None of these systems beat the house edge. The maths is independent each round; past results don't predict future ones. The scoreboards are decorative, not strategic.

Dragon Tiger — The Simplified Asian Variant

Dragon Tiger is a two-position card game where each side gets just one card. Whichever side draws the higher-value card wins. There are no third-card rules, no hand totals — just one card per side. Round resolves in 20–30 seconds.

Trends & Patterns — Common Baccarat Myths

Myth: "The shoe is hot for Banker — keep betting Banker." Reality: a shoe that has produced 10 Banker wins in a row is exactly as likely to produce Banker on the next hand as a shoe that has produced 5-5. The maths is independent. Streak-watching is decoration.
Myth: "Switching between Banker and Player based on the pattern improves results." Reality: every hand is independent. Switching adds nothing. Always Banker is the maths-optimal strategy.
Myth: "Tie bets are worth chasing because they pay 8:1." Reality: Tie probability is ~9.5%, not the 11% needed to make 8:1 a fair bet. Even at 9:1 payout (rare), Tie remains the worst bet on the table.

Baccarat FAQ

What is the lowest house edge in baccarat?

The Banker bet has the lowest house edge at approximately 1.06% — one of the lowest in the entire casino. Player is 1.24%. Tie is a massive 14.4% — avoid it. Even after the 5% commission on Banker wins, Banker remains the best baccarat bet.

What is Dragon Tiger?

Dragon Tiger is a simplified two-card baccarat variant from Asian casinos. Each side (Dragon and Tiger) gets one card; higher card wins. House edge on Dragon or Tiger bet is 3.73%. Faster than standard baccarat — rounds resolve in 20–30 seconds.

Is there a strategy for baccarat?

There are no in-play strategy decisions in baccarat — the rules for drawing a third card are fixed and automatic. Your only choice is which bet to place. The mathematically optimal play is: always bet Banker (1.06% house edge after commission), never bet Tie (14.4%). Betting patterns and "systems" don't change the maths.

Why does Banker pay 0.95:1 instead of 1:1?

The 5% commission compensates the casino for the Banker position's slightly higher win probability (due to the third-card rules favouring Banker). Even after the commission, Banker has a lower house edge than Player.

Should I bet Tie because it pays 8:1?

No. Tie probability is ~9.5%, which makes the fair payout closer to 9.5:1. At the actual 8:1 payout, Tie has a 14.4% house edge — one of the worst bets in the casino. Skip Tie entirely.

What is Speed Baccarat?

Evolution's faster baccarat variant — round time approximately 27 seconds (vs ~48 in Classic). Same RTP, same rules, just faster. Useful if you want more hands per hour. Variance is higher because of the increased round count.

Can I count cards in baccarat?

Theoretically yes, but the edge gained is so small (~0.005% under perfect conditions) that it's not worth the effort. Online baccarat reshuffles frequently, removing any deck-composition advantage entirely.
