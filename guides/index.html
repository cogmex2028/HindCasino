Why State-Level Guides Matter for Indian Casino Players

India has no central law that explicitly governs online casino play. The Public Gambling Act 1867 is federal but pre-dates the internet by over a century and covers physical gambling houses, not online operators. The Information Technology Act 2000 doesn't address gambling. The Consumer Protection Act doesn't either. What fills the gap is state-level legislation — and that's why your physical location in India matters more than your nationality for understanding what's legal where you play.

Maharashtra has the colonial-era Bombay Prevention of Gambling Act 1887 still on the books, but its enforcement against online players is essentially nil — police priorities are physical betting rings, not individual casino accounts. Karnataka attempted a 2021 ban on all online games of skill played for stakes; the Karnataka High Court struck it down. Tamil Nadu's 2022 ban on online rummy and poker was also struck down, then re-enacted with carve-outs. Andhra Pradesh and Telangana have similar evolving bans. Sikkim and Nagaland licence online operators under state frameworks.

For the player, this means the answer to "is online casino legal in my state?" is rarely a clean yes or no. It's a stack of factors: which statute is currently in force, what the most recent court ruling said, how state police are actually enforcing it (often: not at all against individual players), and what your bank's gambling-flagged transaction policy looks like. Our state-by-state guides walk through each of these layers for the largest Indian markets — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi, West Bengal, Tamil Nadu, and Telangana — and reference the actual statute citations and court orders rather than summarising in vague terms.

Tax on Casino Winnings — What Every Indian Player Needs to Know

Tax treatment is a federal question and applies regardless of state. Section 115BB of the Income Tax Act 1961 taxes winnings from "lotteries, crossword puzzles, races including horse races, card games, and other games of any sort" at a flat 30% — plus applicable surcharge and 4% health & education cess. From April 2023, online gaming intermediaries are required to deduct 30% TDS on net winnings at the time of withdrawal or at the end of the financial year, whichever is earlier.

Offshore-licensed casinos based in Curaçao or Malta don't operate under Indian tax jurisdiction and don't deduct TDS. That puts the reporting obligation on the player. The relevant tax form is ITR-2 (if you have other income beyond salary) under "Income from other sources" — the same line where you declare gift income or interest. The 30% rate applies to net winnings (total wins minus total losses in the same financial year), not gross deposits.

We are not tax advisors, and this isn't tax advice. Talk to a CA if you have meaningful winnings to report. Our role is to make sure you know the rules exist — many Indian casino players are surprised to discover that winnings are taxable at all, because Indian casinos themselves rarely highlight it.
