The Short Answer

As of May 2026, Karnataka has no enforceable ban on online casino games. The state's 2021 amendment attempting to ban online real-money games was struck down by the Karnataka High Court in February 2022. The current position relies on the older Karnataka Police Act and the historical legal grey zone for offshore licensed online casinos.

For practical purposes, Karnataka online casino players are in the same legal position as most other Indian states — using offshore licensed casinos without a state-level ban, but also without a state licensing framework that legitimises the activity.

Tax position is separate: 30% TDS on net winnings under Section 115BBJ applies to all Indian residents regardless of state.

The Karnataka Police Act

Karnataka's primary gambling legislation is the Karnataka Police Act 1963 (specifically Chapter VII — Gaming Houses). The original Act:

  • Criminalises running or managing a "common gaming house"
  • Targets operators of gambling establishments, not individual players
  • Was written before online gambling existed and didn't specifically address it
  • Exempts games of skill (consistent with Indian gambling jurisprudence)

For decades, this meant Karnataka was in the same grey zone as Maharashtra, Delhi, and most other Indian states for online casino purposes. Then came the 2021 amendment.

The 2021 Amendment — A Brief Ban

In October 2021, the Karnataka government passed an amendment to the Karnataka Police Act explicitly targeting online gaming. Key provisions:

  • Banned all "wagering or betting on games of skill and chance" via online means
  • Imposed penalties up to ₹1 lakh fine and 3 years' imprisonment
  • Made platforms hosting such activity liable
  • Specifically extended to both chance games and skill games (controversial inclusion)

The amendment received broad criticism. The Indian gaming industry challenged it in the Karnataka High Court, arguing the inclusion of skill games was unconstitutional (violating Supreme Court precedent that skill games are protected under Article 19(1)(g)).

Karnataka High Court Ruling — February 2022

All India Gaming Federation v. State of Karnataka (2022) Karnataka High Court bench struck down the relevant provisions of the 2021 amendment, holding that the state cannot ban games of skill played for stakes. The court found the amendment was overbroad — it criminalised skill games (like Rummy) that the Supreme Court had previously held to be constitutionally protected.

The KHC ruling effectively returned Karnataka to its pre-2021 position. The original Karnataka Police Act provisions remained, but the specific online-gaming ban was nullified. The Karnataka government has not subsequently passed a successor law — making Karnataka, in effect, a state that tried to ban online gaming and failed in court.

Source: All India Gaming Federation v. State of Karnataka, Karnataka HC, Writ Petition No. 18703 of 2021, decided 14 February 2022.

Current Position in Karnataka (May 2026)

Karnataka now operates under the pre-2021 Karnataka Police Act, with the 2021 online-gaming-ban provisions nullified by the KHC. Practical consequences:

  • No active state-level ban on online gambling (chance or skill).
  • Offshore licensed online casinos serve Karnataka players in the same grey zone as Maharashtra, Delhi, and most other Indian states.
  • Indian-based skill-game operators (RummyCircle, Adda52 Rummy) operate legally in Karnataka under the skill-game framework.
  • Enforcement against individual players is effectively absent. Karnataka Police gambling-enforcement focus is on physical satta operations.
  • The Karnataka government could attempt new legislation, but would need to draft it carefully to survive judicial review.

Could Karnataka Re-Ban Online Gambling?

Yes, in principle. The Karnataka government could draft new legislation that addresses the constitutional concerns the KHC raised in 2022. A successful re-ban would need to:

  • Distinguish carefully between skill games (constitutionally protected) and chance games (less protected)
  • Provide a proportionate enforcement framework
  • Avoid the overbroad language that doomed the 2021 amendment

As of May 2026, no specific re-ban legislation is publicly proposed in Karnataka. The political appetite appears low after the 2022 court loss. But Indian gambling law moves fast — this position could change within a single legislative session.
